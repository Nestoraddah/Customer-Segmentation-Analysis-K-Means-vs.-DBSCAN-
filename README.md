Project Description: 
This project explores the application of unsupervised learning to segment customer populations using two distinct approaches: Partition-based clustering (K-Means) and Density-based clustering (DBSCAN). By applying these algorithms to retail (Mall Customers) and financial (Credit Card Usage) datasets, the study evaluates how different mathematical assumptions impact the resulting customer personas. The analysis includes rigorous hyperparameter tuning using the Elbow Method and Silhouette Score Analysis to ensure optimal cluster quality.

Project Goal:

The primary objective is to identify meaningful patterns in customer behavior—specifically income-to-spending ratios and balance-to-purchase habits—to assist in targeted decision-making. A secondary goal is to compare the effectiveness of K-Means and DBSCAN in handling real-world data noise and outliers to determine which algorithm provides more actionable business intelligence.
Core Business Question:

How can a business effectively segment its entire customer base for comprehensive marketing outreach while simultaneously identifying unique outliers that may represent high-risk or high-value anomalies?

Strategic Recommendations:

To optimize business outcomes, use K-Means as the primary tool for broad marketing strategies, specifically targeting five retail segments and three credit card usage tiers to ensure 100% population coverage for growth and retention campaigns. Complement this by implementing DBSCAN as a secondary diagnostic to identify "noise" points, allowing for specialized VIP management or risk mitigation of outliers whose behavior deviates from the norm. Finally, always validate the Elbow Method results with Silhouette Scores during hyperparameter selection to ensure that customer segments are both mathematically distinct and structurally meaningful.
